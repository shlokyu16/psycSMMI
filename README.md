## Overview

This project examines the measurable relationship between social media usage patterns and mental health risk across four domains: ADHD-related attentional difficulties, anxiety, self-esteem disruption, and depressive symptoms. A custom non-linear scoring pipeline transforms 12 Likert-scale survey items into weighted composite domain scores, which feed a suite of machine learning classifiers trained to predict binary mental health risk.

The primary output is a deployed Streamlit application where users complete a 13-question survey and receive a real-time risk prediction from a trained MLP Neural Network (97.9% test accuracy, AUC = 0.9985).

---

## Research Objectives

1. Quantify correlations between social media behavior and four mental health domains: ADHD, Anxiety, Self-Esteem, Depression
2. Design and justify a custom non-linear scoring system that captures sub-clinical signal from Likert-scale responses
3. Comparatively evaluate 8 machine learning models under identical preprocessing and train/test conditions, plus a Keras FNN
4. Deploy the best-performing model in an accessible, interactive web interface

## Methodology

### Dataset

- **Source:** [Social Media and Mental Health — Kaggle](https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/souvikahmed071/social-media-and-mental-health)
- **Raw size:** 481 survey responses, 21 columns
- **Valid responses after cleaning:** 474 (7 rows removed: non-binary gender entries with insufficient representation for encoding)
- **Features:** 20 survey questions covering demographics, social media usage frequency, and 12 Likert-scale mental health indicators
- **Demographic scope:** Primarily university-aged respondents (18-24); includes sex, relationship status, and occupation

### Custom Weighted Grading System

The central methodological contribution of this work is a non-linear remapping of standard 1-5 Likert responses into weighted scores. Rather than using raw ordinal values, each response is transformed as follows.

**Standard questions (ADHD Q1-Q4, Anxiety Q1-Q2, Self Esteem Q1, Q3, Depression Q1-Q3):**

| Likert Response | Meaning              | Weighted Score |
|----------------:|----------------------|:--------------:|
| 1               | Very Positive        | -1             |
| 2               | Slightly Positive    | 0              |
| 3               | Neutral              | 0              |
| 4               | Slightly Negative    | 3              |
| 5               | Very Negative        | 5 (unchanged)  |

**Self Esteem Q2** ("How do you feel about these comparisons?") uses an **inverted scale** in the original survey (1 = Very Negative, 5 = Very Positive). Inversion ensures higher scores always correspond to greater risk:

| Likert Response | Meaning              | Weighted Score |
|----------------:|----------------------|:--------------:|
| 1               | Very Negative        | 5              |
| 2               | Slightly Negative    | 3              |
| 3               | Neutral              | 0              |
| 4               | Slightly Positive    | -2             |
| 5               | Very Positive        | -4             |

**Rationale:** Neutral-to-positive responses are zeroed out to reduce noise from non-committed answers. Moderately negative responses (Likert 4) receive a disproportionate weight of 3, reflecting the clinical reasoning that consistent mild-negative symptom endorsement carries more diagnostic signal than isolated extreme self-reports, which are susceptible to response bias.

### Domain Score Aggregation

Individual weighted scores are summed into four composite domain scores:

- **ADHD Score** = ADHD Q1 + Q2 + Q3 + Q4 (max: 20, min: -4)
- **Anxiety Score** = Anxiety Q1 + Q2 (max: 10, min: -2)
- **Self Esteem Score** = SE Q1 + Q2 + Q3 (max: 15, min: -6)
- **Depression Score** = Depression Q1 + Q2 + Q3 (max: 15, min: -3)
- **Total Score** = sum of all four domain scores

### Binary Outcome Variable and Threshold Selection

The binary outcome (0 = No Risk, 1 = At Risk) is derived from Total Score using a threshold of **22.5**. The selection followed two steps:

1. **Data-driven initial estimate:** KMeans (k=2) clustering on Total Score, evaluated with Youden's J statistic on the resulting ROC curve, yields an optimal cutoff of 17.0. At this threshold, 48.3% of respondents are classified as at-risk, which is substantially above expected population prevalence.

2. **Epidemiological calibration:** WHO (2022) prevalence estimates for combined anxiety and depression in young adults centre around 30-37%. A search over candidate thresholds identified 21.5-22.0 as producing approximately 37.1% at-risk prevalence. The threshold of **22.5** was selected, producing a prevalence of **35.2%** (167 at-risk, 307 no-risk), well-aligned with the epidemiological benchmark.

### Feature Selection

Pearson correlation analysis was used to assess whether demographic features carry meaningful predictive signal:

| Feature             | Correlation with Outcome |
|---------------------|:------------------------:|
| ADHD Score          | 0.71                     |
| Depression Score    | 0.68                     |
| Anxiety Score       | 0.63                     |
| Self Esteem Score   | 0.41                     |
| Time Spent          | 0.36                     |
| Sex                 | 0.09                     |
| Relationship Status | -0.15                    |
| Occupation          | -0.16                    |
| Age                 | -0.20                    |

Demographic features (Age, Sex, Relationship Status, Occupation) show weak correlations with Outcome (|r| <= 0.20), and their directions indicate that social media's effect on mental health risk does not concentrate in any particular demographic group in this dataset. These features were excluded from model training. Total Score was also excluded to prevent target leakage.

**Final model input features:** Time Spent (ordinal 0-5), ADHD Score, Anxiety Score, Self Esteem Score, Depression Score

### Model Comparison

Eight scikit-learn classifiers and one Keras FNN were evaluated under identical preprocessing. Train/test split: stratified 80/20, `random_state=16` (379 train, 95 test).

| Model                   | Hold-out Accuracy | Hold-out F1 | ROC-AUC | CV Accuracy (10-fold)  |
|-------------------------|:-----------------:|:-----------:|:-------:|:----------------------:|
| Logistic Regression     | 1.0000            | 1.0000      | 1.0000  | 0.9957 +/- 0.0128      |
| SVC                     | 0.9895            | 0.9851      | 1.0000  | 0.9832 +/- 0.0158      |
| SGD                     | 0.9895            | 0.9851      | n/a     | n/a                    |
| **MLP Neural Network**  | **0.9789**        | **0.9697**  | **0.9985** | **~0.97 +/- 0.02** |
| KNN                     | 0.9684            | 0.9538      | 0.9968  | 0.9811 +/- 0.0198      |
| Random Forest           | 0.9684            | 0.9565      | 0.9968  | 0.9619 +/- 0.0264      |
| Gaussian NB             | 0.9684            | 0.9565      | 1.0000  | 0.9431 +/- 0.0163      |
| Decision Tree           | 0.9368            | 0.9091      | 0.9304  | 0.9388 +/- 0.0333      |
| FNN (Keras)             | 0.9579            | 0.9412      | 0.9912  | n/a                    |

**Selected model:** `MLPClassifier(solver='lbfgs', alpha=1e-5, hidden_layer_sizes=(5120, 16), max_iter=500)`

Logistic Regression achieves 100% on the hold-out set, a near-linear separability artefact on this dataset size and not a reliable estimate (CV std = 0.013). SVC is the closest legitimate competitor (CV accuracy 0.983), but MLP was selected for deployment because it serialises cleanly with joblib, requiring no TensorFlow dependency at inference time.

## Results

**MLP Neural Network on hold-out test set (95 samples):**

| Class       | Precision | Recall | F1-score | Support |
|-------------|:---------:|:------:|:--------:|:-------:|
| No Risk (0) | 0.98      | 0.98   | 0.98     | 62      |
| At Risk (1) | 0.97      | 0.97   | 0.97     | 33      |
| Overall     | 0.98      | 0.98   | 0.98     | 95      |

- **Test Accuracy:** 97.9%
- **ROC-AUC:** 0.9985
- **Confusion Matrix:** TN=61, FP=1, FN=1, TP=32

One false negative (missed at-risk case) and one false positive out of 95 test samples. Recall for At Risk of 0.97 is the critical metric here — the model misses approximately 3% of at-risk individuals, a clinically relevant consideration.

**Key finding:** ADHD Score is the strongest individual predictor (r = 0.71 with Outcome), followed by Depression Score (r = 0.68) and Anxiety Score (r = 0.63). Time Spent shows a moderate positive correlation (r = 0.36), directly supporting the hypothesis that increased social media consumption contributes to elevated mental health risk.

## Limitations

- **Dataset size:** 474 responses limits generalisability. A larger, more demographically diverse sample would improve reliability.
- **Self-report bias:** Likert-scale surveys are susceptible to social desirability and recall bias.
- **Binary classification:** Mental health risk exists on a spectrum. Future work could explore ordinal regression or multi-label classification across individual domains.
- **Cross-sectional design:** A single-timepoint survey cannot establish causality. Longitudinal data is necessary to determine directionality between social media use and mental health outcomes.
- **Demographic scope:** The sample skews toward university-aged respondents (18-24) and may not generalise to older or non-student populations.
- **Outcome label construction:** The binary label is derived from a custom scoring system, not a validated clinical instrument. This is a deliberate methodological choice but should be noted when interpreting results.
